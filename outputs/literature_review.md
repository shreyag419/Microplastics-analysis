# Literature Review — Microplastics Analysis Project

## Paper 1: [Microplastics in Terrestrial and Freshwater Environments]
**Authors:** Emilie M. F. Kallenbach, Elisabeth S. Rødland, Nina T. Buenaventura & Rachel Hurley
**Year:** 2021
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-78627-4_4
**Source:** Microplastic in the Environment: Pattern and Process

**Summary:**
This chapter studies microplastic pollution in terrestrial and freshwater environments, with emphasis on sources, movement, and environmental impact. It explains that major pollution sources include urban runoff, road dust, sewage sludge, agricultural plastics, and industrial waste. The study describes how microplastics travel through soil, rivers, and sediments, eventually accumulating in ecosystems. It also highlights difficulties in detection, monitoring, and lack of standard sampling methods. The findings support the use of pollution indicators, spatial mapping, and environmental datasets for identifying contamination hotspots. TThis is relevant to our project because it helps explain how plastic waste from Delhi and Mumbai can move through drainage systems into rivers and eventually marine environments, supporting our marine microplastics dataset analysis and GIS hotspot mapping.

**Key Finding:**
Land-based human activities are the main drivers of microplastic pollution, so urban and industrial indicators can be useful predictors of high-risk areas.

## Paper 2: [Microplastic pollution in soil and groundwater: a review]
**Authors:** Rogers Wainkwa Chia, Jin-Yong Lee
**Year:** 2021
**Link:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/S10311-021-01297-6
**Source:** Environmental Chemistry Letters

**Summary:**
This review focuses on microplastic pollution in soil and groundwater systems, which are less studied than oceans but equally important. It identifies sources such as plastic waste dumping, agricultural films, landfill leakage, and wastewater contamination. The paper discusses harmful effects on soil fertility, plant growth, microorganisms, and possible human exposure through water systems. It also reviews treatment approaches like filtration, biodegradable materials, and improved waste management. The study emphasizes the importance of monitoring contamination using data-driven methods and environmental risk assessment. This is relevant to our project because it directly supports the soil pollution dataset by showing how waste dumping, landfill leakage, and urban plastic use can contaminate land and groundwater, which can be analyzed using clustering and prediction models.

**Key Finding:**
Microplastic pollution in soil and groundwater is strongly linked to human land use patterns, making predictive modelling and hotspot detection valuable for prevention.

## Paper 3: [Mini-review of microplastics in the atmosphere and their risks to humans]
**Authors:** Guanglong Chen, Qingyuan Feng, Jun Wang 
**Year:** 2020
**Link:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048969719354981
**Source:** Science of The Total Environment

**Summary:**
This review article examines microplastic pollution in the atmosphere, highlighting air as an emerging pathway of plastic contamination. It explains that airborne microplastics mainly come from synthetic textiles, plastic waste breakdown, industrial emissions, traffic activity, and dust resuspension. The study discusses how these particles travel long distances through wind and settle in urban as well as remote environments. It also raises concerns about human exposure through inhalation, especially in indoor spaces where concentrations may be higher. The paper emphasizes the need for better monitoring methods and pollution source identification. This is relevant to our project because it connects airborne microplastics with traffic, industrial emissions, and urban dust, making the Delhi and Mumbai AQI datasets useful indicators for studying city-based pollution risk.

**Key Finding:**
Urban and industrial human activities are major contributors to airborne microplastics, making city-based environmental indicators useful for pollution risk modelling.One line.

## Paper 4: [Environmental exposure to microplastics: An overview on possible human health effectsTitle here]
**Authors:** Joana Correia Prata, João P. da Costa ,Isabel Lopes, Armando C. Duarte, Teresa Rocha-Santos
**Year:** 2019
**Link:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048969719344468
**Source:** Science of The Total Environment

**Summary:**
This review article provides an overview of how humans are exposed to microplastics through air, food, drinking water, and daily consumer products. It explains that ingestion and inhalation are the two major exposure pathways, especially in urban environments with higher plastic use and pollution levels. The study discusses possible health effects such as inflammation, oxidative stress, tissue damage, and transport of toxic chemicals attached to plastic particles. It also highlights current limitations in measuring exposure levels and the need for better monitoring methods. The paper emphasizes linking environmental contamination data with human risk assessment models. This is relevant to our project because it shows why identifying pollution hotspots matters, since highly polluted urban, soil, and marine regions may increase human exposure through air, water, and food chains.

**Key Finding:**
Human exposure to microplastics is strongly connected to polluted urban environments, making predictive environmental risk mapping important for prevention and policy planning.

## Overall Conclusions
The four research papers collectively show that microplastic pollution is strongly influenced by urbanization, industrial activity, waste generation, and population density. These findings directly support our project analysis of Delhi and Mumbai datasets, where AQI and city pollution indicators help represent environmental stress linked to airborne microplastics. The marine microplastics dataset helps validate how land-based urban pollution can eventually enter rivers and oceans through runoff and drainage systems. Similarly, the soil dataset supports the connection between waste mismanagement and land contamination risks. Overall, these studies justify our use of machine learning, clustering, and GIS mapping to identify pollution hotspots and predict high-risk zones across air, soil, and marine environments.
